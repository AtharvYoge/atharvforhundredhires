## Where Experts Disagree

---

## Disagreement 1: How long should a webinar be?

Russell Brunson recommends: 90 minutes minimum. His "Perfect Webinar"
framework is built around a long teaching section followed by a detailed
offer stack. He argues that longer webinars pre-sell the audience so
thoroughly that conversion rates are higher even if attendance drops off.

Joel Erway recommends: 45 minutes maximum, often 20–30 minutes for
B2B SaaS specifically. He calls this the "micro-webinar" and argues that
B2B buyers have less tolerance for long sessions and shorter formats
outperform in show-up rate and attendee-to-buyer ratio.

My take: Joel Erway is right for B2B SaaS specifically.
Russell Brunson built his framework selling to entrepreneurs and consumers
who are emotionally invested in transformation. B2B SaaS buyers are
evaluating tools, not changing their lives. They have 30 minutes, not 90.
A 45-minute webinar with a tight 10-minute pitch converts better for
this audience than a 90-minute marathon where half the attendees drop
off before the offer.

## Disagreement 2: Should you offer a replay?

Amy Porterfield recommends: Always offer a replay, available for 48–72
hours. Replays capture the 40–60% of registrants who could not attend
live, and replay buyers convert at nearly the same rate as live attendees
if the follow-up sequence is strong.

Jason Fladlien recommends: No replay, or a replay only available for
purchase. Scarcity of a live-only event dramatically increases show-up
rates. Free replays train your audience to skip the live session.

My take: Amy Porterfield is right for early-stage B2B SaaS.
When you are running your first webinars and building an audience,
maximising the number of people who see your content matters more than
maintaining scarcity. Scarcity is an optimisation tactic, not a
starting-from-zero tactic.

## Disagreement 3: Should you pitch on the webinar at all?

Rand Fishkin recommends: Educational-first, no hard pitch. The best B2B
webinars build long-term trust and pipeline over months. SparkToro's
webinars end with a soft product mention, not a structured close.

Russell Brunson and Jason Fladlien recommend: A structured 15–20 minute
pitch with offer stacking, bonuses, and a deadline. Not pitching is
leaving money on the table.

Nathan Barry's approach: Webinars were educational and gave away real
value, but always ended with a clear free trial offer. No hard close,
but no invisible product either.

My take: It depends on price point and sales cycle.
Under $500/year self-serve SaaS — use a direct pitch. It works.
Enterprise or anything requiring a sales conversation use Rand Fishkin's
or Nathan Barry's approach. End with "start a free trial" or "book a call,"
not "buy now before midnight."
