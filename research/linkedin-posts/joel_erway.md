## Post 1
Date: [1 March 2026]
Link: [https://www.linkedin.com/posts/joelerway_i-had-a-sales-call-the-other-day-and-reminded-activity-7434624196627939328-Y50R?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADV5al8BQgB4-ooi4L8oI09iRGyq9rxBA1A]

I had a sales call the other day and reminded me about why some agencies hit $30K/month and stay there for years... like I was.

It's not ad strategy. Most agency owners know what good ads look like.

It's not client acquisition. If you can get to $30K, you can find more clients.

The bottleneck is almost always the same thing: creative production can't keep pace with media buying insights.

Your team finds three winning angles in the data this week. Great. Now you need 15 new ad variations to test those angles across different formats and copy approaches.

If you're running a traditional shop, that's a 2-3 week turnaround minimum. Maybe longer if your designer is slammed or your copywriter is juggling five clients.

Meanwhile, you're burning budget on ads you already know aren't optimal.

I built my entire automation system around eliminating this exact friction point. 

Not because I love technology. Because I was tired of the constant cycle of needing to drop everything when client campaigns need refreshing.

The math is simple: If you can deploy new creative the same day you spot the pattern, you capture the momentum. If you deploy three weeks later, you're buying expensive validation of what you already knew.

Most agencies lose more money to production lag than they lose to bad targeting.

If you're an agency or launching your own ads... how long does it take you to go from idea to live?

## Post 2
Date: [2 March 2026]
Link: [https://www.linkedin.com/posts/joelerway_had-a-call-last-week-with-an-agency-owner-activity-7433163061911531520-FbiX?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADV5al8BQgB4-ooi4L8oI09iRGyq9rxBA1A]

Had a call last week with an agency owner in France. She manages Meta ads for eight or nine clients, mostly in the health space. Workshop funnels, mix of German and US markets.

She does everything herself. No team behind her.

I asked how much time she spends on ad creative every month and she said somewhere between 16 and 24 hours. 

That's potentially three full working days just making ads for her clients.

Then she said something that I've heard so many times over the last 13 years it almost feels scripted at this point. 

She said when a campaign starts dropping in performance mid-launch, she has to "leave everything behind" and go all in on producing new creative. Everything else stops.

This is the story of almost every agency owner I talk to. The details are different but the core problem is identical.

Nobody is struggling with strategy. Nobody is struggling with media buying. 

Agency owners are generally really, really good at that stuff. 

The thing that puts a ceiling on the business is always creative production.

Because there's no system behind it. It's manual. It's time-intensive. And it scales linearly with the number of clients you take on. 

So you hit 8, maybe 10 clients and you're maxed. Not because you can't manage more accounts, but because you can't physically make enough ads.

I lived this for years running my own agency. I tried every version of solving it with people. In-house designers, freelancers, outsourced agencies. 

The problem wasn't the people. The problem was that the entire creative production workflow was fundamentally broken. 

Every new client just meant more manual work on top of more manual work.

What actually unlocked the next level for me was when I stopped trying to hire my way out of it and started building systems that removed me from the process entirely. 

That's how I got to $220K a month. Not by working more. Not by hiring more. By building smarter.

I think about that call with the agency owner in France and I just wish someone had told me this 10 years ago: if your agency requires more of your time to grow, the problem isn't effort. It's infrastructure.

The best agencies right now aren't the biggest. They're the ones that solved creative production at scale without adding headcount.

Before AI, we blamed time.

Now we blame AI.

Truth is? The problem’s always been us.

“I don’t have enough time to post every day!”

That used to be the excuse.

Now it’s:

“Everyone’s posting generic AI content!”

But here’s the truth…

If you struggled before AI, you’ll struggle after it.

Unless you fix the root issue.

It’s not about tools.

Or time. Or prompts.

Most people aren’t blocked by ideas.

They’re blocked because they lack a process.

One of my mentors shared a Zig Ziglar quote:

“Find something good to say. Say it well. Say it often.”

That stuck.

Most people kinda know what to say.

But they don’t know how to say it well.

And they definitely don’t say it enough.

So they try to outsource everything.

Voice. Beliefs. Strategy. Soul.

That’s not content creation.

That’s nuking your brand.

AI should support your voice.

Not replace it.

The sooner you stop trying to automate your soul..

The sooner you’ll start creating content that actually lands.

## Post 5
Date: [9 March 2026]
Link: [https://www.linkedin.com/posts/joelerway_in-2013-i-joined-my-first-entrepreneurship-activity-7343624035802468352-oIBR?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADV5al8BQgB4-ooi4L8oI09iRGyq9rxBA1A]

In 2013, I joined my first entrepreneurship group.

(Took me 11 years to actually start the SAAS LOL)

The first lesson they gave me?

It wasn’t to hire developers…

Or to learn code…

Or to create a mockup…

They gave me a folder of hundreds of proven sales letters.

Gary Halbert, John Carlton, Gary Bencivenga…

The legends. 

Then they told me:

“Print them out and copy them by hand.”

Literally, word-for-word with pencil in a binder. 

Honestly, I was a little pissed. 

I paid $6K to start a SAAS, not become a copywriter.

But the money had left my bank account so I gave it a shot.

Day 1: my hand cramped.

Day 2: I started to notice patterns.

Day 3: the hooks started clicking.

Day 4: I could feel what made a line punch.

By Day 7…

I was thinking like a writer.

That’s when it hit me:

IMMERSION is the fastest way to get great at anything. 

Even writing copy. 

From rhythm… to the structure… to the hooks… the flow…

All of it matters. 

You train your eye to spot what works.

And more importantly you build taste, style, and tone. 

If you’re stuck trying to find your “voice”...

Start by borrowing one that already works.

And make it yours through repetition.

You don’t need more creativity. 

You just need more reps.
