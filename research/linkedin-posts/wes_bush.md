## Post 1
Date: [14 April 2026]
Link: [https://www.linkedin.com/posts/wesbush_we-asked-edith-harbaugh-founder-of-launchdarkly-activity-7449465927332630529-B6Bv?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADV5al8BQgB4-ooi4L8oI09iRGyq9rxBA1A]

We asked Edith Harbaugh, founder of LaunchDarkly, why she came back as CEO.

“My job as a founder is to make sure the team is having fun.”

That might sound soft.

It’s actually one of the sharpest leadership points in our conversation.

Are your people feeling the satisfaction of producing something meaningful?

Whether engineers are shipping.
Whether PMs are making progress.
Whether designers are creating.
Whether sales and marketing can hear a customer say: “LaunchDarkly made my life so much better.”

That’s a very different way to think about a founder’s job.

And honestly, I think it matters even more right now.

AI is changing how fast teams can build. Markets are moving faster. Customer expectations are rising.

In that kind of environment, the best founders create the conditions where talented people can do their best work and enjoy doing it.

That idea showed up throughout the conversation:

1. LaunchDarkly didn’t win by forcing a category into existence.
They listened closely to how teams were releasing software and met them where they were.

2. They kept a self-serve motion even while building an enterprise business.
Because developers want to experience the product before talking to sales.

3. Edith sees AI as a way to remove toil.
Less time stuck in low-leverage work.
More time building, learning, and creating.

4. The strongest founders care deeply about the problem.
That genuine empathy is what keeps them going when things get hard.

If you’re building in SaaS or leading product teams, this one is worth your time.

Thanks for coming on Edith!

## Post 2
Date: [13 April 2026]
Link: [https://www.linkedin.com/posts/wesbush_your-pricing-page-might-be-costing-you-more-activity-7447276056987873280-4Pkx?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADV5al8BQgB4-ooi4L8oI09iRGyq9rxBA1A]

Your pricing page might be costing you more than your homepage.

Why?

I’ve looked at hundreds, if not thousands, of pricing pages over the years.

Most pricing pages make that decision harder than it should be.

The ones that convert well usually do a few things extremely well.

The weak ones tend to create hesitation right when a user is closest to buying.

If I were tearing down your pricing page, here’s the simple checklist I’d use:

1. Is it instantly clear who each plan is for?

“Pro”
“Business”
“Enterprise”

Those labels mean very little on their own.

A good pricing page helps users self-identify fast.

For example:

Starter → for individuals getting started 
Growth → for small teams scaling usage 
Enterprise → for companies with security and admin needs

If users have to guess where they fit, they slow down.

2. Is the value metric obvious?

Per seat? 
Per workspace? 
Per usage? 
Per outcome?

If someone needs to read your FAQ to understand what they’re paying for, that’s a problem.

Clarity beats cleverness every time here.

3. Are you leading with features or outcomes?

Most pricing pages turn into a giant feature spreadsheet.

But buyers are trying to answer a simpler question:

“What will this help me do?”

Your job is to connect each plan to the outcome it unlocks.

4. Is there a clear upgrade path?

A good pricing page makes expansion feel natural.

Users should understand:

- what they get now 
- what they unlock next 
- when it makes sense to move up 

Confusion here hurts both conversion and expansion revenue.

5. Are you creating friction at the worst possible moment?

This one gets missed all the time.

If your page says “Get started” but the CTA takes users into a sales funnel, trust drops fast.

If a plan looks self-serve, let it be self-serve.

And if it requires sales, explain why.

6. Are you helping users choose, or making them work?

Too many options can hurt conversion. 
Too many add-ons can hurt conversion. 
Too much comparison detail can hurt conversion.

A pricing page should reduce decision fatigue, not increase it.

7. Is your pricing page answering the real objections?

Not every objection belongs on a sales call.

Good pricing pages proactively address things like:

- billing flexibility 
- cancellation 
- implementation 
- security 
- usage limits 
- overage concerns 

The best pricing pages remove doubt.

## Post 3
Date: [6 April 2026]
Link: [https://www.linkedin.com/posts/wesbush_saas-is-dead-is-one-of-the-laziest-takes-activity-7446937907141070849-eiIa?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADV5al8BQgB4-ooi4L8oI09iRGyq9rxBA1A]

“SaaS is dead” is one of the laziest takes on the internet right now.

Nick Franklin doesn’t buy it. SaaS platforms are actually becoming more valuable.

He’s been in SaaS for 17 years and lived through the shift from on-prem to cloud, the COVID boom, the ZIRP hangover, and now the AI reset.

His view: This has been the most disruptive 3-year stretch in SaaS history. But it may also be the most exciting.

Why?

Because AI is compressing the time between idea and execution.

For product-focused founders, that changes everything.

You can:

- ship faster
- test more ideas
- increase output without scaling headcount the same way
- raise the bar on how quickly users get to value

That last part matters most.

The old version of SaaS could get away with making the user do too much work.

The new standard is very different.

Users expect the product to do more of the heavy lifting.

That creates pressure.

But pressure is often where the best companies get built.

In our conversation with Esben Friis-Jensen and me, Nick also shared:

1. Why so many AI startups are still buying classic SaaS
2. Why SaaS platforms are becoming more valuable, not less
3. How ChartMogul is thinking about AI, moats, and the future of subscription analytics
4. Why “just build the best product and keep customers happy” still matters more than most strategy decks

My favorite quote: “Your biggest MOAT in AI is to make your customers consistently happy.”

Thanks for coming on, Nick Franklin!


## Post 4
Date: [3 April 2026]
Link: [https://www.linkedin.com/posts/wesbush_the-gpu-gold-rush-how-vastai-scaled-with-activity-7444401771764035585-MivS?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADV5al8BQgB4-ooi4L8oI09iRGyq9rxBA1A]

Vast.ai’s growth caught me off guard.

In the past 12 months:

- Paid customers up 16x
- Sign-ups up 27x

A lot of that growth is coming from a place most companies don’t fully understand yet.

When people ask LLMs how to rent a GPU, they often get pointed to Vast.ai. That alone is driving a huge amount of traffic and adoption.

But what stood out even more was the team.

They have around 44 people.

They are not hiring. Not for support. Not for engineering.

And it’s not because they need to be careful with money. The business is doing very well.

They are choosing to stay lean.

That led to a bigger question.

What happens when companies can scale revenue without scaling headcount?

With AI and a strong product-led motion, more customers don’t always mean more people. In some cases, the opposite seems to happen. The product improves, the system gets more efficient, and growth compounds without adding much overhead.

Some of the fastest-growing teams right now are starting to question whether hiring more people would actually slow them down.

Feels like a shift.

Curious if others are seeing the same thing?

## Post 5
Date: [1 April 2026]
Link: [https://www.linkedin.com/posts/wesbush_founders-think-theyre-competing-against-activity-7443666284161990656-0Q4r?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADV5al8BQgB4-ooi4L8oI09iRGyq9rxBA1A]

Founders think they’re competing against other products.

Most are actually competing against a shrinking clock.

Every market has an expected Time to Value. And that expectation keeps compressing.

I call this The Halving Principle™.

The Halving Principle™ is the observation that a market's expected Time to Value compresses every few years through incremental improvements, then collapses almost overnight when AI eliminates entire steps from the user's workflow.

The gradual halvings are PLG 1.0 improvements. The cliff is PLG 2.0.

Here’s what I mean by that ⤵️

Your market's expected Time to Value halves every few years. But it doesn't halve on a neat schedule.

It halves gradually.

Better onboarding here, a smoother flow there. And then it collapses almost overnight when AI hits your category.

What actually changes is the entire workflow instead of speed to value.

Making a presentation used to be five steps: outline, build slides, design, edit, finalize.

Now it's two: outline, verify. Three steps vanished. Legal research went from "days with Westlaw" to "ask Harvey and review the answer."

Here's the exercise you can do now:

Open your product right now. Start a stopwatch. How long until a brand-new user sees a real output.

I mean a real output. Not a dashboard, or a welcome screen, or a configuration wizard, but something they'd actually pay for?

That number is your Time to Value.

Now halve it.

Can your product deliver that?

Because someone in your market is building toward it this quarter. And when they ship it, your users won't complain. They'll leave.
